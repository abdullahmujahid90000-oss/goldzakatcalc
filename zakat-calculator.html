Cash Zakat Calculator Saudi Arabia 2026 — How Much You Owe

Zakat is the third pillar of Islam — a religious obligation for every Muslim adult whose wealth exceeds the Nisab threshold and remains in possession for one full lunar (Hijri) year. In Saudi Arabia, Zakat is also a regulated financial duty enforced on businesses and certain individuals by the Zakat, Tax and Customs Authority (ZATCA), the same body that runs VAT. Whether you're paying voluntarily or filing with ZATCA, understanding the Nisab, the 2.5% rate, and what counts as Zakatable wealth is essential. Use the calculator above to find your obligation, then read the guide below for the complete rules.

What is Zakat and who must pay it?

Zakat (الزكاة) is the obligatory annual giving of 2.5% of a Muslim's eligible wealth to specified categories of recipients listed in the Quran (Surah At-Tawbah 9:60): the poor, the needy, those employed to collect Zakat, those whose hearts are to be reconciled, those in debt, those in slavery, those in the cause of Allah, and travellers in need. Every adult Muslim of sound mind whose wealth exceeds the Nisab threshold for one full lunar year must pay.

In Saudi Arabia, the Zakat law distinguishes between individuals (who pay voluntarily through charitable channels) and businesses (who file mandatory Zakat returns with ZATCA at 2.5% on the company's Zakat base — broadly, equity + long-term funding minus fixed assets). This calculator focuses on the individual obligation.

Nisab — the threshold below which no Zakat is due

Nisab is set in the Sunnah at either 85 grams of pure gold or 595 grams of pure silver. Most scholars in 2026 use the gold Nisab for cash wealth because it gives a higher threshold (and so requires fewer marginal payers to give Zakat). At an indicative Saudi gold price of SAR 305/g for 24K, the gold Nisab in 2026 sits at roughly SAR 25,925. The silver Nisab, at roughly SAR 4 per gram, is about SAR 2,380 — much lower. Use whichever your local scholar or madhab recommends; in Saudi Arabia the gold standard is mainstream.

The 2.5% formula and a worked example

Zakat = (Total Zakatable wealth − debts due within the year) × 2.5%
Only if the result of (wealth − debts) ≥ Nisab

Worked example: Your Zakat anniversary is 1 Muharram 1448 AH. On that date you hold: SAR 35,000 in your Saudi savings account, SAR 5,000 in physical cash at home, SAR 12,000 of business inventory you are selling, USD 2,000 (= SAR 7,500) in a foreign account, and a SAR 4,000 car loan instalment due within 12 months. Zakatable wealth = 35,000 + 5,000 + 12,000 + 7,500 = SAR 59,500. Subtract the immediate debt: 59,500 − 4,000 = SAR 55,500. Since this exceeds the Nisab of SAR 25,925, Zakat is owed: 55,500 × 2.5% = SAR 1,387.50.

What is Zakatable and what is NOT

Zakatable (pay 2.5% on):

  • Cash in any currency, in bank accounts or at home
  • Gold and silver — including jewellery worn occasionally
  • Investment shares and unit trusts (use market value on your anniversary date)
  • Cryptocurrency (mainstream scholarly view in 2026)
  • Business inventory held for resale — at market value
  • Receivables certain to be paid (e.g. a friend's loan they will repay)
  • Income from rented properties — but not the property itself if held long-term

NOT Zakatable:

  • Your primary home and household belongings (clothes, furniture)
  • Your personal car or commute vehicle
  • Tools and equipment used in your job/business
  • Office buildings, factories, productive assets — only their rental income is Zakatable
  • End-of-Service (EOS) gratuity not yet received
  • Pension contributions not yet vested
  • Personal jewellery used regularly (according to one main view; some scholars include all gold)

When and how to pay Zakat in Saudi Arabia

Pick a Zakat anniversary date — many people use the first day of Ramadan, but any Hijri date you remember works. Once wealth exceeds Nisab on that anniversary AND has stayed above Nisab for the preceding 12 lunar months, Zakat is due. Pay through licensed Saudi Zakat channels such as the National Platform for Charitable Work (Ehsan, ehsan.sa), the Saudi Red Crescent, or directly to eligible categories. Receipts from registered charities are accepted by Saudi banks as proof for HR/visa documentation requirements where relevant.

Frequently asked questions

Do non-Muslim expats in Saudi Arabia pay Zakat?

No. Zakat is a religious duty exclusive to Muslims. Non-Muslim residents and businesses are not required to pay Zakat — but Saudi corporations with foreign shareholders may still owe Zakat on the Saudi/GCC-owned portion and corporate income tax on the foreign-owned portion to ZATCA.

Can I pay Zakat in instalments?

Yes. The full amount is due on your anniversary date, but you can pay it over weeks or months without sin, as long as you actually pay the calculated amount in full and don't delay without genuine financial hardship. Some scholars permit paying Zakat in advance (before the lunar year completes), which is useful in Ramadan.

Do I deduct my long-term mortgage from Zakatable wealth?

The mainstream view is to deduct only the instalment payments due within the next 12 months from your Zakatable cash. You do not deduct the entire mortgage balance, because the underlying property (your home) is not Zakatable anyway and the long-term loan does not reduce the cash you have on your anniversary date.

Does cryptocurrency count for Zakat?

Most contemporary scholars in 2026 — including the AAOIFI Shari'ah Board and the Islamic Fiqh Academy of the Muslim World League — treat cryptocurrency as a Zakatable asset, valued at its market price on your Zakat anniversary. Pay 2.5% in either crypto or its SAR equivalent.

Is my GOSI pension contribution Zakatable?

Not until you can actually receive it. While you are still working and GOSI contributions are locked away, they are not in your possession and Zakat is not due. Once you retire and start receiving payments, those payments enter your bank account and become Zakatable cash for that lunar year if total wealth exceeds Nisab.

Can I give Zakat to my parents or siblings?

You may NOT give Zakat to anyone you are financially obligated to support — that includes your parents, your spouse, and your children (because giving them money is a duty, not charity). You may give Zakat to siblings, uncles, aunts and other relatives if they qualify as poor or needy under the eight Zakat categories.
