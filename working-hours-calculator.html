Saudi Working Hours & Overtime Rules 2026 — A Practical Guide

Saudi Labour Law sets specific rules for working hours, rest periods, overtime pay and Ramadan timing. These rules apply to every private-sector employee — Saudi or expat, full-time or part-time — and override anything in your individual contract that's less generous. Knowing your entitlements protects you from being underpaid for legitimate overtime, and helps employers stay compliant. The calculator above computes hours and hourly rate; the guide below explains the law behind every number.

Saudi standard working hours

  • Standard working week: 48 hours, typically 8 hours/day × 6 days/week, or 9 hours/day × 5 days plus a short Saturday.
  • Maximum: 48 hours per week. Any hour beyond is overtime.
  • Mandatory rest break: at least 30 minutes after every 5 consecutive working hours. Cannot be skipped.
  • Weekly rest day: Friday is the default rest day. A different day can be designated only with the employee's written consent (common for retail, hospitality, healthcare).
  • Ramadan working hours: reduced to 6 hours/day, 36 hours/week for Muslim employees. Non-Muslim employees keep their normal schedule unless the employer applies a blanket reduction.

Overtime — the 150% rule

Under Article 107 of the Saudi Labour Law, any hour worked beyond the standard daily or weekly cap must be paid at 150% of the regular hourly rate — that's the base rate plus 50% premium. The formula is:

Hourly rate = (basic salary + housing allowance) ÷ (working days × hours/day)
Overtime pay = hourly rate × 1.5 × overtime hours

Worked example: An employee with basic SAR 6,000 + housing SAR 1,500 = SAR 7,500/month works the standard 26 days × 8 hours = 208 hours. Hourly rate = 7,500 ÷ 208 = SAR 36.06. If they work 10 hours of overtime in a month: overtime pay = 36.06 × 1.5 × 10 = SAR 540.90. That's added to the gross salary for that month.

Rest day & public holiday overtime — the 200% rule

Working on your designated weekly rest day (typically Friday) or on a public holiday (National Day, Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, Founding Day) triggers a double rate — 200%. If your employer doesn't pay double, they must compensate with a paid day off the following week. You can't simultaneously demand both (the law gives the employer the choice).

Caps and limits on overtime

Saudi law caps total overtime at 720 hours per year, or roughly 60 hours per month on average. Beyond that, the employer must obtain special authorisation from HRSD. Most legitimate use cases (project deadlines, seasonal retail peaks, maintenance shutdowns) fit well within this cap. Routine 70+ hour weeks indicate either employer non-compliance or a contract issue.

Common overtime mistakes employers make

  • Forgetting housing allowance. Hourly rate is calculated on basic + housing, not basic only. Excluding housing under-pays overtime by 20–30%.
  • Paying overtime as a "fixed allowance". Some companies fold overtime into a flat monthly bonus. This is not compliant — overtime must be hour-for-hour.
  • "Salaried, so no overtime". Saudi labour law has no exemption for "managerial" or "salaried" employees. Anyone under contract is entitled to overtime if they work beyond 48 hours per week.
  • Counting Ramadan against the cap. During Ramadan the standard week is 36 hours for Muslim employees, so the threshold for overtime drops too — anything past 36 hours that week is overtime, not just past 48.

What if I'm not being paid overtime?

Track every hour you work — most modern offices have biometric or app-based attendance. Take a screenshot or photo of the system at the end of each month. If overtime hasn't been paid, raise it in writing with HR. If still unresolved after 14 days, file a complaint at hrsd.gov.sa or call the labour hotline 19911. Unpaid overtime claims can be filed up to 12 months after the work was performed — past that the right is forfeited.

Frequently asked questions

Does my contract override the legal working-hour rules?

A contract can offer MORE than the law (e.g. 40-hour week, 200% overtime, extra rest day) but never LESS. Any contract clause that gives the employee less than the legal minimum is automatically void — the legal standard prevails.

Are remote / work-from-home hours subject to the same rules?

Yes. Working hours, breaks, weekly rest, and overtime entitlements apply whether you're in the office or at home. Track your hours via your company's time-tracking system or your own log.

Can I refuse overtime?

Generally yes — overtime requires mutual agreement. Exceptions exist for urgent operational needs (safety, emergency repair, preventing damage). For routine project deadlines, you can decline without disciplinary consequence, although in practice this depends on workplace culture.

Do part-time employees get overtime?

Yes, in proportion to their part-time hours. If your contract specifies 25 hours/week, hours 26–48 are regular pay (top-up), and beyond 48 is overtime at 150%. The 48-hour weekly cap applies the same regardless of contract type.

How are night-shift hours treated?

Saudi law doesn't mandate a night-shift premium for adults (only for women, who may not be required to work between 11pm and 6am except in certain authorised sectors). Many employers voluntarily pay a 10–25% night-shift allowance, but it's contractual not legal.
