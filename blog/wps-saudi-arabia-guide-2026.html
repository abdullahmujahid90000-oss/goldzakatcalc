Saudi WPS (Wages Protection System) 2026 — The Complete Guide

Everything every employee and every employer in Saudi Arabia needs to know about WPS — by what date your salary must arrive, how to check your status, what happens to your employer if they're late, and what you can do if you don't get paid.

Quick summary: WPS (Wages Protection System / نظام حماية الأجور) is the Saudi government's mandatory salary-tracking program. Every private-sector employer must pay every employee through the banking system by the 3rd day of each Gregorian month. If your employer misses the deadline, the Ministry of Human Resources (HRSD) automatically blocks them from issuing new work visas, transferring Iqamas or renewing commercial licences. As of 2024 the system applies to every company, even those with a single employee.

1. What is WPS, and why does it exist?

The Wages Protection System — usually shortened to WPS in English, or نظام حماية الأجور in Arabic — is a system built and operated by the Saudi Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development (HRSD, formerly known as MOL or MoL). It was first rolled out in 2013 and has been progressively tightened every year since.

The system has one very simple goal: make it impossible for an employer to "forget" to pay an employee. Before WPS, a worker who didn't receive their salary on time had to file a complaint with the labour office, wait for an investigation, and try to prove the unpaid wages with paper documents. Hundreds of thousands of low-wage workers — especially construction labourers and domestic workers — never bothered, because the process was too slow and too risky for their visa status. Wage theft was rampant.

WPS fixes this by routing every salary through the banking system. The employer uploads a monthly salary file (or has their bank do it via the Mudad platform), the bank pays each employee, and HRSD receives a real-time copy of every transaction. The moment a payment is late or the wrong amount, HRSD knows automatically — no complaint required.

This matters to you whether you're an employee or an employer. If you're earning a salary in Saudi Arabia, WPS is what stands between you and unpaid wages. If you're running a company, WPS is what determines whether you can sponsor new employees, transfer Iqamas, or renew your commercial licence.

3. The salary payment deadline rule

This is the single most important rule in the entire system, so memorise it:

Every salary must arrive in the employee's bank account by the 3rd day of the following Gregorian month.
Example: salary for May 2026 must be in your bank account no later than 3 June 2026.

A few subtle but critical details:

  • Gregorian calendar, not Hijri. Even though most other Saudi government deadlines follow the Hijri calendar, WPS uses the Western (Gregorian) calendar. Employers used to the Hijri system regularly miss this and end up in Yellow zone for the first time.
  • "By the 3rd" means by the end of the 3rd day. A transfer initiated late on 3 June at 23:30 still counts as on-time. But a transfer initiated at 00:01 on 4 June is officially late.
  • The full salary must be transferred. Partial payments — for example, paying basic salary but withholding the housing allowance — count as a partial payment violation. Your employer must transfer the full contractual amount as registered with the Qiwa portal.
  • The deadline applies even if you didn't work the whole month. If you joined mid-month, you should still be paid (pro-rata) by the 3rd of the following month.

4. The Yellow / Red WPS zones explained

WPS uses a colour-coded compliance system, similar to Nitaqat. Every employer is assigned a zone based on their payment history:

ZoneTriggerWhat happens to the employer
GreenAll salaries paid in full, by the 3rd of the following month, for the last 6 months.Full government services available. Can sponsor new employees, transfer Iqamas, renew commercial licences.
YellowOne or more salaries late by 4–30 days, or partial payment.Warning level. Employer is notified by HRSD. Cannot transfer Iqamas from other companies inward.
RedSalaries late by 31+ days, or repeated Yellow violations.Severe restrictions: no new work visas, no Iqama transfers in or out, no commercial licence renewal, no Saudization-based government benefits. Effectively frozen.

The colour-coded WPS zone interacts with the Nitaqat Saudization zones separately — an employer can be Platinum in Nitaqat but Red in WPS, in which case the WPS restrictions still apply.

5. How to check your own WPS status (employee guide)

If you want to see exactly when each of your salaries arrived in your account according to the government's records (not your bank statement, but the official HRSD record), here's how:

Method 1 — Mudad (recommended for employees)

  1. Go to mudad.com.sa and click "Employee Login" (or "تسجيل دخول الموظف").
  2. Enter your Iqama number and the password sent to your registered mobile number.
  3. Click "Salary History" (or "سجل الرواتب") in the left menu.
  4. You'll see a month-by-month list with: date salary was transferred, amount transferred, payer (employer), and a status badge (On Time / Late / Partial).

Method 2 — Absher

  1. Log in to absher.sa using your Iqama number.
  2. Go to "My Services" → "Labour Services" → "Wage Protection".
  3. You will see the same data as above, plus your employer's current WPS zone (Green / Yellow / Red).
Tip: Make a habit of checking your WPS status on the 4th of every month. If your salary for the previous month isn't showing as "On Time", your employer has officially missed the deadline and you can take action — see section 7.

6. How to set up WPS as an employer

If you're a Saudi or expat employer, here's the quick setup:

  1. Register on Mudad. Visit mudad.com.sa, choose "Employer Sign-up", and link your commercial registration (CR) and Establishment Number from the Ministry of Commerce.
  2. Add your employees. Each employee's contract must already exist in Qiwa (the labour ministry's contract registry). Mudad pulls names, Iqama numbers, and contractual salary amounts directly from Qiwa.
  3. Authorize your bank. Most major Saudi banks (Al Rajhi, SNB, Riyad, ANB, Alinma) are integrated with Mudad. You authorize the bank to debit salary totals from your corporate account and distribute to employee accounts on a schedule you set.
  4. Process the monthly salary file. Each month, on or before the 3rd, you upload the salary file (or use Mudad's recurring template) and your bank executes the payments. Mudad sends the transaction record to HRSD automatically.

If you're a small employer who just hired your first employee — common for new SMEs and start-ups — the simplest path is to ask your bank's relationship manager to enable "Mudad Salary Service". The bank does most of the setup work and the monthly cost is typically SAR 5–15 per employee.

7. What to do if your employer is late or skips WPS

If the 3rd of the month passes and your salary hasn't arrived, follow this exact sequence:

  1. Day 4 (one day late): Send a polite written message to your HR or direct manager asking for an update. Keep a screenshot of the message.
  2. Day 7 (4 days late): Check Mudad. If the status is "Pending" or "Failed", the issue may be a technical bank rejection (e.g. your IBAN is wrong). Update your bank details in Mudad and notify HR.
  3. Day 10 (one week late): File a complaint at the Ministry of Human Resources. Either visit hrsd.gov.sa → "File a Labour Complaint", or call the Labour Hotline 19911. You'll need your Iqama number, your employer's name, and the period of unpaid wages.
  4. Day 30 (one month late): Your employer is now automatically in Red zone. You can escalate the case to the Labour Court (Mahkamat al-Amal), or if your contract is short you can claim early termination with the wages owed plus the end-of-service gratuity due to you. Use our EOS Calculator to know the exact amount.
Important: Your right to file a WPS complaint is protected — your employer cannot retaliate by cancelling your Iqama, refusing to renew it, or banning you from re-entry. If they try, this is itself a separate, much more serious labour violation.

8. Why cash salaries are now illegal

It's still surprisingly common in 2026 to hear stories of small employers — especially in retail, small restaurants and household-run shops — handing employees an envelope of cash on the last day of the month and asking them to sign a paper receipt. This is illegal under the current WPS rules. Here's why both sides should care:

  • For the employer: Any salary not transferred through WPS is treated as unpaid. Even if the employee signed a receipt, HRSD does not recognise cash payments. The employer is automatically flagged as "non-compliant" and the standard Yellow/Red penalty kicks in. The cash they handed over is essentially gone from the books and the legal liability remains.
  • For the employee: If a dispute arises later — for example, when calculating end-of-service gratuity, or claiming GOSI contributions — the only salary that HRSD recognises is the WPS-recorded salary. If the WPS record shows SAR 1,500/month but you were actually receiving SAR 5,000 in cash, your EOS is calculated on SAR 1,500. You lose two-thirds of your gratuity.

The lesson for employees: never accept cash payments instead of WPS, and never let your employer transfer some portion of your salary "off the books" to a separate account. The GOSI and EOS rights you build up are calculated only on what WPS sees.

9. How WPS connects to GOSI, Iqama and Nitaqat

WPS doesn't operate in isolation. It's tied to almost every other employment system in the kingdom:

WPS ↔ GOSI

Your GOSI contributions (9.75% from your salary if you're Saudi, 0% if expat) are calculated on the contractual salary registered with Qiwa, which is the same salary WPS pays. If your employer pays you less through WPS than what your Qiwa contract says, GOSI also under-calculates your contributions, which reduces your pension. Verify your GOSI contributions monthly using our GOSI calculator.

WPS ↔ Iqama renewal

An employer in Red WPS zone cannot renew employee Iqamas. Even if you, the employee, have paid the SAR 650 renewal fee on time, your sponsor must still be in good standing for the renewal to process. Use our Iqama expiry tracker to spot upcoming renewals early.

WPS ↔ Iqama transfer

If you want to move to a new employer, both your current employer and the new employer must be in Green WPS zone. A common stuck case: an employee finds a better job, but their current employer is in Yellow zone because of someone else's late salary, blocking the transfer. Our Iqama transfer calculator includes a WPS zone eligibility check.

WPS ↔ Nitaqat

Nitaqat (Saudization) zones and WPS zones are independent but related. A company can be Platinum in Nitaqat (lots of Saudi employees) but still Red in WPS (late salaries). In that case, the WPS restrictions override the Nitaqat privileges. Read more in our Nitaqat Saudization guide.

10. Five common WPS mistakes that cost employers Iqama transfers

  1. Confusing Hijri and Gregorian deadlines. The Hijri month can end on a different date than the Gregorian month. WPS uses Gregorian. Employers who pay based on Hijri month-end are often 2–3 days late under WPS rules.
  2. Salary contract vs. WPS amount mismatch. If the contract in Qiwa says SAR 5,000 and the WPS transfer is SAR 4,500, even though the employee agreed verbally, this is a partial-payment violation.
  3. Treating bonuses as salary. Mid-year bonuses, profit shares and one-off allowances should NOT be paid through the regular WPS salary line. They should be flagged as a separate "additional payment" in Mudad. Mixing them inflates the WPS-tracked salary and creates a permanent obligation to pay the higher amount monthly.
  4. Forgetting employees on unpaid leave. If an employee is on unpaid leave for a full month, the employer must still mark them as such in Mudad. Leaving the field empty triggers a "missing salary" alert.
  5. Using a personal bank account. The corporate account used for WPS must be a registered business account, not the owner's personal account. Mudad-linked banks now reject personal accounts automatically, but legacy setups sometimes slip through and cause Yellow-zone flags.

11. Frequently asked questions

What if my employer asks me to sign a paper saying I was paid in cash?+
Don't sign. Cash receipts are not legally recognised by HRSD. The only salary that counts for your GOSI, EOS and Iqama records is the WPS transfer. If your employer insists, this is a strong signal that they're not paying GOSI on your behalf either — both serious violations.
I'm a freelancer with a Freelance Permit. Do I use WPS?+
No. WPS only applies to employees on a regular employment contract registered in Qiwa. Freelancers issue invoices to their clients and receive payment as regular business income. You should still pay your own GOSI contributions if you opt into the freelancer GOSI scheme.
What if my salary is in USD or another foreign currency?+
All WPS payments must be in Saudi Riyals (SAR). If your contract specifies a foreign currency, the SAR equivalent at the official Saudi Central Bank (SAMA) rate on the payment date is what gets transferred and tracked.
Can my employer deduct money from my WPS salary?+
Only for specific reasons defined in Saudi Labour Law: GOSI (Saudis only), authorised loans you signed for, court-ordered deductions, or unrecovered cash advances. The total deduction cannot exceed 50% of monthly wages. All other deductions are illegal.
How long does it take to fix a Red WPS zone?+
Once all outstanding salaries are paid in full, the employer moves to Yellow zone immediately. Moving from Yellow back to Green requires 6 consecutive months of on-time, full payments. There's no shortcut.
Does WPS apply to part-time workers?+
Yes. If a part-time worker has a formal Qiwa contract (which they must, to be legally employed), they're entitled to WPS protection just like a full-time employee. The salary amount is whatever the Qiwa contract specifies.
I work for a foreign company without a Saudi entity — does WPS apply?+
If you're physically working in Saudi Arabia and being paid for that work, you need a Saudi work visa and Iqama, which means you need a Saudi employer or sponsor, which means WPS applies. If you're a remote employee billed as a freelance contractor by a foreign company, that's a different (and legally grey) area — consult a Saudi labour lawyer.
