About Musaned Platform

Musaned (مساند) is the official Saudi government platform for recruiting and managing domestic workers. All domestic worker recruitment must go through Musaned or licensed agencies.

Minimum wages vary by nationality and are set by bilateral agreements between KSA and the worker's home country.

Employer responsibilities: Provide accommodation, meals, healthcare, and annual ticket home. The domestic worker is exempt from the expat dependent levy.

Musaned Domestic Worker Cost Guide 2026 — Visa, Salary, Levy & Insurance

Musaned (مساند) is the Saudi government platform that regulates hiring domestic workers — housemaids, drivers, nannies, cooks, and farmers — from approved source countries. Bringing a domestic worker to your home in Saudi Arabia in 2026 carries hidden costs well beyond the headline visa fee: monthly salary, recruitment fees to the source-country agency, mandatory medical insurance, the new domestic-worker WPS wallet, annual Iqama, and a one-time levy. This calculator gives you the bottom line. The guide below walks through every line so you don't get a surprise bill.

Who can hire through Musaned?

Any Saudi citizen, or any expat resident with a valid Iqama and monthly income above SAR 8,000 (SAR 6,000 if hiring a male worker), can sponsor a domestic worker. Higher-income brackets are allowed multiple workers — typically one worker per SAR 6,000 of monthly income above the threshold, up to a household maximum of four. You apply entirely online at musaned.com.sa, with your Saudi address verified through Absher and your salary verified via Mudad or your employer's official letter.

The 2026 cost breakdown — what you actually pay

  • Visa issuance fee: SAR 2,000 one-time, paid to the Saudi government through Sadad when the worker's visa is approved.
  • Recruitment-agency fee (source country + Saudi): typically SAR 8,000–15,000 depending on origin and skill level. Filipino workers cost more (SAR 12,000–18,000) than Bangladeshi or Sri Lankan (SAR 7,000–12,000). This fee covers training, medical screening, ticket and agency profit.
  • Monthly salary: the regulatory minimums in 2026 are SAR 1,500/month for Filipina housemaids, SAR 1,200 for Sri Lankan, SAR 1,000 for Bangladeshi/Indonesian, SAR 1,400 for Indian drivers. Most families pay slightly above the minimum to attract and retain good workers.
  • Domestic worker WPS wallet: SAR 5 per month — the new mandatory salary-protection channel. Salaries must be paid through this wallet (not cash), starting 2024.
  • Annual Iqama renewal: SAR 600/year covers Iqama renewal and the labour card.
  • Mandatory medical insurance: SAR 1,200–2,000 per year, depending on the worker's age and gender.
  • Round-trip ticket (every 2 years): the employer must pay the worker's flight home for annual leave — budget SAR 2,000–4,000 every 24 months.
  • Food and accommodation: by law you must provide both in-kind. Typical monthly cost: SAR 500–800.

Worked example — total cost of a Filipina housemaid for 2 years

Visa fee SAR 2,000 + Agency fee SAR 14,000 + Salary 24 × 1,500 = SAR 36,000 + WPS 24 × 5 = SAR 120 + Iqama 2 × 600 = SAR 1,200 + Insurance 2 × 1,500 = SAR 3,000 + Return ticket SAR 3,500 + Food 24 × 600 = SAR 14,400
Total 2-year cost: SAR 74,220 — or about SAR 3,093/month all-in.

The first-year cost is heavier (because of one-off recruitment and visa) — roughly SAR 50,000 — and the second year drops to about SAR 24,000. This is why most agencies push families toward 2-year contracts.

Source countries — pros and cons in 2026

  • Philippines — best English ability and customer service. Higher salary (SAR 1,500+) and higher agency fees. Wait time: 2–4 months.
  • Indonesia — recently reopened after years of restrictions. Strong reputation. Salaries SAR 1,200–1,500.
  • Sri Lanka — popular for nannies and cooks, moderate cost (SAR 1,200), reliable.
  • Bangladesh — cheapest option (SAR 1,000–1,100), shortest wait time (4–6 weeks). Limited English.
  • Kenya / Uganda — newer corridors opened 2023, mid-range cost (SAR 1,100–1,400).
  • India — primarily drivers (SAR 1,400+); for housemaids only via special bilateral arrangements.

Frequently asked questions

Can I pay my domestic worker in cash?

No, not since 2024. All domestic-worker salaries must be paid through the Musaned wallet (essentially a WPS-equivalent for household employers). Cash payment is treated as unpaid salary by HRSD, exactly like for regular employees. The wallet is funded from your bank account and the worker withdraws via their own debit card.

Can I transfer a domestic worker from another employer?

Yes. Musaned added inter-employer transfer in 2022. Both employers must agree, the transfer fee is SAR 2,000, and the worker themselves must consent in writing through Absher. Transferring an existing worker saves you the visa fee and recruitment fee — total saving of around SAR 10,000–15,000.

Do I have to pay the dependent levy on my domestic worker?

No. The SAR 400/month dependent levy applies only to your sponsored family members (spouse, children, parents), not to domestic workers. Domestic workers have their own separate fee structure. Use our Dependent Levy Calculator for the family side.

What if my domestic worker absconds (runs away)?

Report immediately to Musaned (within 7 days) by filing an "Absence" report. This protects you from being liable for the worker's actions after they left. After 30 days of confirmed absence, you can apply for a replacement worker without paying a new visa fee. Failing to report on time can leave you legally responsible for the worker's stay or any incidents.

How many weekly rest days do I have to give?

Saudi domestic-worker regulations require one rest day per week and 9 hours of daily rest including 8 hours of continuous overnight sleep. You also owe one month of paid annual leave per year of service plus EOS gratuity (½ month per year, up to 5 years; 1 month per year thereafter). The same Article 84 formula as regular employees — calculate yours with our EOS Calculator.

Is medical insurance for the worker mandatory?

Yes — fully employer-paid medical insurance is mandatory under the Council of Cooperative Health Insurance (CCHI). Policy must cover hospitalisation, emergency care, and basic outpatient. Cost is around SAR 1,200–2,000 per year and must be renewed annually with Iqama renewal.
